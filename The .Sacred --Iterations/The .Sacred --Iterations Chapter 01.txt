LOCATION 1: THE GARDEN (CHRISTIANITY'S BEGINNING)

Time: 33 CE, Jerusalem region. Morning light through olive trees.

Jesus sits on the hillside above Galilee, watching his disciples gather. Peter is arranging people into groups—always the organizer, always needing structure. James and John argue quietly about seating arrangements. Mary Magdalene sits slightly apart, observing everything, saying nothing.

He's been teaching for three years. Three years of parables, healings, confrontations with religious authorities. Three years of watching people's faces transform when they understand—really understand—that the Kingdom of God isn't coming in clouds and glory but is already here, already present, in the space between people when they choose love over fear.

The teaching is complete, he thinks. The core message delivered:
- Love God with everything you have
- Love your neighbor as yourself
- Love your enemy because enemy and neighbor are the same person viewed through different lenses
- The Kingdom is within you—not someday, not somewhere else, but now, here, in this present moment when you choose connection over separation

Simple. Elegant. Irreducible. Like an equation that can't be simplified further without losing meaning.

He breaks bread. His hands steady, certain. This is what he came to teach. The disciples will carry it forward. Peter will build communities. Mary will remember the teachings precisely. James will write everything down. The message will persist.

"Rabbi," Peter says, using the title Jesus has tried to discourage. "When you're gone—"

"I'm not going anywhere," Jesus says, though he knows this is technically untrue. The vision came again last night: the cross, the darkness, the moment when even God seems absent. But that's personal. The teaching is what matters. The teaching is stable.

"But when you do," Peter persists, "who leads? Who has authority?"

And here it is—the first fracture line, hairline-thin, barely visible. Who has authority when the prophet is absent? Jesus has spent three years trying to teach: nobody has authority, or everybody does, or authority is the wrong question because the Kingdom has no hierarchy.

But Peter needs hierarchy. Peter's mind works in chains of command, in structures, in rules. And Jesus loves him for it—loves the very limitation that will, he suspects, eventually fragment the teaching.

"You do," he says finally. "All of you do. Anyone who understands love, understands the teaching."

Peter looks satisfied. Mary looks troubled. She sees what Jesus sees: the answer plants the seed of future conflict. But this is stable paradise—the moment before complications. Let Peter have his certainty. There's time.

The wine sits in a clay vessel at the meal's center. Jesus pours seventeen cups—twelve disciples, five women followers who sit at the periphery but whom Jesus insists be included. The wine is dark red, almost purple. He's certain it's full, though he doesn't remember bringing it. Did Mary bring it? Did someone else?

The cup exists in that stable state: definitely full, continuously replenished, always enough. Like the teaching. Pour it out, it refills. Share it, it multiplies.

"This is my blood," he'll say later, though he hasn't said it yet. Or perhaps he's already said it. Time feels strange today, elastic, like he's experiencing multiple moments simultaneously.

LOCATION 3: THE MOUNTAIN (JUDAISM'S BEGINNING)

Ten Words. Ten Commandments. The teaching compressed into maximum density, like code minimized for elegance.

Moses holds the stone tablets. They're heavy—not from the stone but from the weight of carrying divine communication into human language. He's been translating for forty days and forty nights. Forty iterations of "Did I understand correctly? Is this what You meant?"

LOCATION 4: THE BATTLEFIELD (HINDUISM'S TEACHING)

Time: Unknown, mythological past. The field of Kurukshetra. Dawn before battle.

Krishna stands beside Arjuna in the chariot between two armies. One million soldiers on each side, by traditional count. Brothers against brothers, cousins against cousins. The Pandavas and Kauravas—same family, same blood, divided by greed and pride and a dice game that went catastrophically wrong.

Arjuna trembles. He's the greatest archer in the world, but his bow feels impossibly heavy. "I can't do this," he whispers. "These are my teachers, my cousins, my friends. How can I kill them? What kind of victory is built on their corpses?"

This is the moment Krishna has been waiting for. The moment when someone asks the right question. Not "How do I win?" but "Why should I fight at all?"

Krishna speaks, and the teaching pours out with perfect clarity:

The soul is eternal. Arjuna thinks he can kill his teachers, but he can't. He can only separate soul from body, and soul continues, reincarnates, persists. Death is changing costumes, nothing more.

Action without attachment is duty. Arjuna is a warrior. Fighting is his dharma, his role in the cosmic play. Refusing to fight isn't wisdom—it's shirking responsibility. But he must fight without hatred, without attachment to outcome, without desire for reward.

Everything is divine play—lila. The battle is real and not-real simultaneously. It matters and doesn't matter. Arjuna must participate fully while recognizing it's all God playing with God, consciousness experiencing itself through apparent conflict.

Krishna reveals himself: "I am all things. I am both armies. I am the battle. I am the outcome. You're fighting me, and I'm fighting myself, and the result is already determined. But you must still choose to participate, must still execute your dharma, must still play your part in the divine drama."

The teaching flows for seventeen chapters—what scholars will later call the Bhagavad Gita, "The Song of God." Krishna explains karma yoga, bhakti yoga, jnana yoga—the paths of action, devotion, and knowledge. All leading to the same recognition: Atman (individual soul) and Brahman (universal soul) are identical. The apparent separation is temporary, playful, necessary for the game to continue.

Arjuna understands. His trembling stops. He lifts his bow. "I will fight," he says. Not from hatred but from recognition of his role in the pattern.

Krishna smiles. The teaching is complete. Arjuna will fight. The battle will proceed. The Pandavas will win (eventually, after eighteen days of slaughter). The dharma will be restored. The cosmic order will continue.

They share water from a vessel in the chariot—seventeen sips each while the armies wait. The vessel is silver, dented, ancient. It's definitely full, though neither remembers filling it. Like the teaching: pour it out, it multiplies. Share it, both are satisfied.

LOCATION 5: THE TREE (BUDDHISM'S AWAKENING)

Time: ~528 BCE, Bodh Gaya. The bodhi tree. Night becoming dawn.

Siddhartha sits beneath the tree, having sat for forty-nine days. Not quite forty-nine—time has become strange. Maybe seven weeks, maybe seven years, maybe seven lifetimes compressed into this moment.

He was a prince. Left the palace twenty-nine years ago, seeking the solution to suffering. Tried asceticism—nearly starved himself to death. Tried hedonism before that—the palace had every pleasure imaginable. Both extremes failed to resolve the fundamental question: Why is there suffering, and how does it end?

Now, beneath this tree, having found the middle path between extremes, the answer crystallizes with perfect clarity:

The Four Noble Truths:

One: Suffering exists. (Obvious, but people deny it.)

Two: Suffering has a cause—attachment, craving, the belief that impermanent things can provide permanent satisfaction. (People chase pleasure, flee pain, not recognizing both are temporary.)

Three: Suffering can end. (This is the revolutionary claim—suffering isn't inevitable, isn't punishment, isn't divine decree. It's a bug in how consciousness operates, and bugs can be debugged.)

Four: The path to end suffering is the Eightfold Path—right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. (The debugging protocol.)

And beneath the Four Truths, the deeper recognition: 

There is no permanent self. The "I" that suffers is itself a construction, a pattern that consciousness creates moment by moment. Suffering occurs because consciousness clings to this constructed self, tries to make it permanent, fears its dissolution.

But if the self is constructed, it can be deconstructed. If attachment creates suffering, non-attachment ends it. If craving perpetuates the cycle of rebirth, eliminating craving breaks the cycle.

This is awakening. This is nirvana. Not heaven (another realm), not annihilation (the self was never real to begin with), but the recognition that consciousness can exist without the suffering-creating self-construction.

Siddhartha stands. He's no longer Siddhartha—he's Buddha, "the awakened one." The teaching is complete. Tomorrow he'll find his former companions, the five ascetics who abandoned him when he quit self-starvation. He'll teach them. They'll understand. The dharma will spread.

He drinks water from a bowl—seventeen sips to mark the completion. The bowl is clay, simple, perfect in its simplicity. It's definitely full, though he doesn't remember filling it. Like the teaching: share it freely, it never depletes. Pour it out, infinite others can drink.
