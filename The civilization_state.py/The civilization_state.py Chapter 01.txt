LOCATION 3: INDUS VALLEY - MOHENJO-DARO (~2500 BCE)

The Great Bath. Noon. The city at its height.

The city planner (name unknown—Indus script still undeciphered) inspects the drainage system beneath the Great Bath. Perfect engineering: every house has indoor plumbing, sewage flows through covered drains to collection points outside the city, the Great Bath itself fills and empties through precisely calculated channels.

Mohenjo-daro is not an empire in Sargon's sense. No palaces. No obvious king. No military fortifications. Instead: perfect order through urban planning. Every street a perfect grid. Every brick a standardized size (ratio 1:2:4—length, width, height). Every house with access to water and sanitation.

The stability comes not from conquest but from engineering. The Indus River floods annually like the Nile, but less predictably. So Mohenjo-daro builds flood defenses, granaries to store surplus, trade networks to buffer against famine. The civilization has solved scarcity through infrastructure.

Seventeen major cities, all built to identical specifications. You could walk from Harappa to Mohenjo-daro (640 kilometers) and find the same street layout, the same brick dimensions, the same drainage systems. This is stability through standardization, but unlike Sargon's military standardization, this is voluntary—cities adopt the pattern because it works.

The planner makes notes on a clay tablet in Indus script. The script that no one in the future will be able to read, because this civilization will collapse so completely that even their language disappears. But the planner doesn't know that. The planner believes the system is stable.

"The flood defenses are holding," an engineer reports. "The granaries are full. Trade with Mesopotamia is profitable. The city prospers."

The planner nods. This is what civilization looks like when you engineer it correctly. Not warfare, not conquest, not divine kings—just good design. Solve the water problem, solve the food problem, solve the waste problem, and humans will thrive.

LOCATION 4: CHINA - THE XIA DYNASTY (~2070 BCE, legendary/mythological)

The Yellow River. The palace of Yu the Great. The flood control project complete.

Yu stands on the bank of the Yellow River, watching it flow between the levees he spent seventeen years building. The river they called "China's Sorrow" because it flooded catastrophically every few decades, killing millions, destroying civilizations. Yu tamed it.

The legend says Yu worked so hard he passed his house three times and never entered, never rested, devoted entirely to the flood control project. Likely exaggeration. But the achievement is real: thousands of kilometers of channels, dikes, levees, transforming the Yellow River from civilization-destroyer to civilization-enabler.

And for this, the previous emperor (Shun) abdicated in Yu's favor, establishing the principle that will define Chinese civilization: the Mandate of Heaven. Rule belongs to whoever can maintain order, prevent disasters, ensure prosperity. When a dynasty loses the mandate (signaled by floods, famines, rebellions), Heaven withdraws approval and a new dynasty rises.

Yu has the mandate. The river flows controlled. The harvests are abundant. The people prosper. The Xia Dynasty is founded on competence—on the ability to solve civilization's fundamental problems.

"The system is stable," his chief engineer reports. "The channels require maintenance, but the design is sound. The flood risk is managed. We've solved the problem."

Yu nods. He believes it. The river is controlled. Future generations will maintain the channels. The mandate is secure. The dynasty has found the formula: prove your competence through hydraulic engineering, maintain legitimacy through results, ensure stability through technical mastery.

Athens. The Agora. Early morning. The assembly gathering.

Solon stands in the Agora—the marketplace, the assembly ground, the heart of the emerging polis.
